CASE FILE 004 / WEB3 MARKETPLACE

A trust-first marketplace in a category where confusion is the default setting.

Built and launched LastKnown, a curated NFT marketplace that hosted the Billion Buns drop — 888 pieces of digital art with a strong cultural pull. The harder problem wasn't aesthetic; it was making confusing crypto flows feel inevitable, and making first-time buyers feel safe.

01 / The Category Problem

By 2022, "NFT marketplace" already meant something specific to most people — and not in a good way. Volatile prices, screaming homepages, opaque fees, and onboarding flows that assumed everyone already owned a wallet. We started LastKnown with a contrarian premise: most of the people we want to sell to have never bought an NFT, and the ones who have are tired of being yelled at.

02 / Editorial, Not Casino

The visual language we landed on was closer to a magazine than a marketplace. Every collection got a short editorial introduction. Every artist got a real bio, written like a profile. Pricing was prominent but never blinking. The drop page treated the work as work — not as ticker symbols. This wasn't a stylistic choice; it was a trust-building move. People treat artifacts that are introduced to them seriously differently than they treat scrolling thumbnails.

"The platform should make a serious collector feel respected, and a first-time buyer feel safe enough to spend $80."

03 / Designing the Onboarding That Wasn't There

Most marketplaces start with "Connect Wallet" — which is a hostile sentence to anyone who doesn't already have one. We built a parallel path for first-time buyers: a clear plain-language explanation, a vetted wallet recommendation, and embedded fiat onramps for users who didn't want to leave the site to figure out how to fund a purchase. Crypto-native users could still connect in two clicks; new users got a guided 3-step path that didn't try to teach them blockchain.

04 / Fees, Said Out Loud

On every checkout we showed: the artwork price, the platform fee, the gas estimate, and the total — before connecting a wallet. No scroll. No expand-to-reveal. The actual numbers, in a row, in plain text. This single decision — which felt almost banal in the design reviews — was the most-quoted moment in our first month of launch feedback.

05 / The Billion Buns Drop

For the launch we partnered with the Billion Buns project — 888 pieces of culturally-rooted digital art — and used the platform's editorial framing to introduce it the way you'd introduce a gallery show. The drop sold out within hours. More important than the speed: the customer-support volume in week one was a fraction of what we'd budgeted for, because the flow itself answered most of the questions.

06 / What Carries Forward

  • Plain-language defaults. Nothing technical until the user has earned the right to see it.
  • Editorial framing. A collection is a story, not a wall of thumbnails.
  • Honest fees. Disclosure is a design pattern, not a footnote.
  • Two paths in. One for natives, one for newcomers — never one watered-down compromise.
