CASE FILE 003 / SERVICE + POS

The unglamorous UX everyone touches.

A complete redesign of the BCLC lottery retail terminal — the point-of-sale software running on every gas station, convenience store, and grocery counter in British Columbia. 4,200 devices, hundreds of thousands of transactions per day, and a real person on each side of the screen.

01 / The Counter is the Product

People don't see the lottery terminal as a piece of software. They see a cashier, a screen, a printer, and a line behind them. The product surface isn't the screen alone — it's the interaction between two people, mediated by hardware, with a queue applying steady pressure. That reframe changed everything about how we approached the redesign.

The old terminal was a Windows application from another era — dense, modal, and built around the database schema rather than the cashier's workflow. New hires took two weeks to feel comfortable. Mid-shift mistakes were expensive: reprints, refunds, frustrated customers, and a queue that never stopped.

02 / Field Research at the Counter

We rode along on retail visits across BC — Tim Hortons in Prince George, gas stations in Surrey, grocery stores in Vancouver. We watched the same handful of mistakes happen over and over again: misreading a play-slip orientation, scanning the wrong section of a winning ticket, fumbling between cash and card while a customer corrected their numbers.

Almost every mistake came from the screen demanding attention at the wrong moment — a modal popup while the cashier was looking at the customer, a confirmation step buried two screens deep, a status indicator that didn't change colour fast enough.

"The cashier never gets to look at the screen for more than a second at a time. Design for that second."

03 / Design Principles

  • One-glance status. Every screen tells you what to do next without reading.
  • Forgiving inputs. Wrong-orientation play-slip? The terminal figures it out.
  • No confirmation theatre. Reversible by default; confirm only when reversal is impossible.
  • High-contrast surfaces. The terminal lives under fluorescent light; the UI has to survive glare.
  • Audio cues that mean something. A different sound for "scan failed" vs. "winning ticket detected."

04 / Training & Rollout

We knew the new terminal would only succeed if we trained 12,000 part-time cashiers across thousands of retail partners. So we designed the UI to be its own onboarding: contextual hints in the first 50 transactions, a "show me the keys" overlay, and an opt-in slow-mode for new operators. Training time dropped by more than half. First-day confidence — measured via a brief in-app survey — went up by 22%.

05 / What Shipped

A complete rebuild of the terminal interface, deployed to all 4,200 devices over a six-month staggered rollout. Average transaction time dropped 38%. Cashier-reported confidence climbed sharply. And the queue behind the counter moved a little faster, which is the only metric that ever really mattered.
