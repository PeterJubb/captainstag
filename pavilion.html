Brief

Not a pergola.
A small room
for water.

The site sits next to a small backyard sauna in Vancouver. After a hot session, the body needs cold, then warm again, then quiet. This pavilion is the architecture of that sequence — a covered cold-plunge enclosure at one end, a standing transition in the middle, an outdoor shower at the other.

Closer to a hidden onsen than a backyard cover — three walls, an open front, and a quiet roof that holds the rain off the wood.

The structure is honest and exposed. Four 6×6 Douglas fir posts carry doubled 2×8 beams. A bank of 2×6 rafters spans the short way and lifts the roof clear of the head. Three sides are clad in vertical tongue-and-groove fir to match the sauna; the fourth is left open to the garden and dressed only with a noren curtain at the head.

The roof is the one piece of industry in the building — matte black corrugated metal on a slight rear slope, fastened above the rafters with shadow gaps so that, from below, only the wood is read. Everything else is the warmth and grain of stained fir, the soft heat of one wall sconce, and the steady percussion of Pacific rain.

Construction sequence

Building it, in order.

01
Set the footprint

Square out the 11' × 8' pad next to the sauna. Anchor four 6×6 standoffs to the slab — keep the post bases off the ground for rot resistance. Re-check diagonals: corner to corner should match within 1/8".

02
Raise the posts

Cut all four 6×6 posts to 8'-0", plus an extra 1/2" at the front pair for the rear roof slope (or scribe later). Stand each post in its standoff and brace temporarily — laser-plumb all four faces.

03
Hang the beams

Sandwich each pair of posts with doubled 2×8 beams — long beams (12') on the front and rear faces, side beams (10') tucked between. Through-bolt with 1/2" galvanized hardware. The pavilion stops racking the moment all four beams are home.

04
Lay the rafters

Drop six 2×6 rafters across the 8' span, ~2' on centre, with the rear ends set ~4" below the front to throw the roof toward the back. Notch a shallow seat over each beam. Tail the front ends with a clean cut — no decorative scrolls.

05
Sheathe & roof

2×4 purlins across the rafters, peel-and-stick membrane over the top, then corrugated metal in matte black with matching closure strips. A hidden gutter along the rear catches the runoff and pipes it to a french drain.

06
Strap the walls

1×3 furring vertically over a breathable WRB on the rear, left, and partial-right framing. This gives the cladding a ventilated cavity — essential for fir holding up in Vancouver winters.

07
Clad in fir

1×6 T&G fir, run vertically, blind-nailed through the tongue. Pre-stain all six faces of every board before installation so any shrinkage doesn't expose raw wood. Leave a small shadow gap at the bottom for drainage.

08
Fit the interior

Cedar duckboard on sleepers over the gravel bed. Bench against the rear wall. Plumb the cold and hot lines for the shower, set the plunge tub on its pad, install the sconce, hang the noren. Burn a candle, sit on the bench, listen.

SheetA5.0
On atmosphere

A place built
for the ritual,
not the view.

Done right, the pavilion disappears into use. The fir darkens with the seasons. The metal roof ticks under hard rain. Steam from the shower lifts out the open front and is gone before it touches the cladding. The cold plunge takes the heat out of you in twenty seconds and leaves quiet behind.

It is not a structure that wants to be photographed — it is a structure that wants to be used at dusk, in November, barefoot, with a cup of tea. The rest of the architecture sits down so the ritual can happen.
