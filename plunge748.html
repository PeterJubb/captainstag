A backyard pavilion · how to build it

Onsen,
by hand.

A complete construction guide for an 11 × 8 outdoor bath pavilion — heavy timber frame, horizontal cedar cladding, a translucent roof for the long winter light, a cold plunge and a hot shower. Built across five weekends with a circular saw, a drill, and one good neighbour.

Before you commit · 01

What you're signing up for.

It is a real build. Not hard — but real. Plan on five productive weekends spread across a month or two, plus another two for plumbing, finishes, and the inevitable second trip to the lumberyard.

Difficulty
Intermediate
If you've built a deck or framed a wall, you have the skills. The trickiest moments are squaring the frame and dropping the polycarbonate sheets cleanly.

Two for the pad and frame, one for the roof, one for cladding, one for plumbing and fit-out. Add a buffer weekend you'll definitely use.

A0.2 Prep Work
Before you commit · 02

Six things to do before lifting a board.

01

Check your setbacks

Vancouver allows accessory structures under 10 m² without a permit in most zones, but rear-yard and side-yard setbacks still apply. Call your district planning desk. Five minutes, saves grief.

02

BC One Call before digging

Free locate service for buried utilities. Three business days' notice. 1-800-474-6886 or clickbeforeyoudig.com. Don't skip this for a small pad — gas lines don't care about your project size.

03

Sun and prevailing rain

Plan the open front to face south or southwest. The solid rear wall takes the prevailing winter weather (here, off the ocean, that's southeast). Stand in the proposed footprint at 4 pm in February if you can.

04

Drainage and grading

Pad slopes 1.5% to the shower drain. Around the perimeter, plan for a 12″ river-rock band that catches splash and routes water away. If you can tie into existing storm drainage, do it now while you're already digging.

05

Lead times

Order the plunge tub three weeks out. Polycarbonate sheets often need to be ordered in too. The 6×6 Doug fir posts are usually in stock at any larger yard — but check before you load up the trailer.

06

One helper

Most of this is solo work. But for raising the corner posts and dropping the doubled beams on top, you want a second pair of hands. Schedule that weekend now, not later.

A4.0 Build Sequence
The build · 10

Six phases, five weekends.

The order matters. Pad before frame, frame before roof, roof before walls (so you stay dry while you work), walls before plumbing rough-in, plumbing before tile and fit-out. Trust the sequence.

01
Site & concrete pad
Weekend 112 hours · 2 people

The least glamorous and most important weekend. A flat, well-drained pad is the difference between a structure that lasts twenty years and one that's racking in two.

1.1 · Mark out
Lay out the footprint

String lines and batter boards. Pad goes 11′-0″ × 8′-0″ outside-of-form. Square the corners with the 3-4-5 method or a long diagonal check (13′-7½″ across the 11×8). Mark for the river-rock band, an extra 12″ all around.

1.2 · Excavate
Dig down 8 inches

Strip topsoil, dig out 8″ across the whole pad area. Get the bottom roughly flat with a slope of about ¼″ per foot toward the future drain location. The rest of the slope is in the slab itself.

1.3 · Base
Compacted gravel base

Lay 4″ of ¾″ minus crushed gravel. Rake flat, wet down lightly, run a plate compactor over it in two passes at right angles. A well-compacted base is what keeps your slab from cracking.

1.4 · Forms
Set forms & vapour barrier

2×4 PT forms staked every 3 ft. Drop in 6 mil poly. Lay welded wire mesh on small chairs (or stones) so it sits in the middle of the slab thickness. Mark anchor-bolt positions on the form tops — you'll set them in wet concrete.

1.5 · Pour
Mix & pour

Mix in batches, fill systematically from one corner. Screed across the forms with a straight 2×4. Bull-float, edge, and set your anchor bolts at exactly the post locations (measure twice). Wait until the slab is firm, then float and trowel smooth.

1.6 · Cure
Walk away

Cover with plastic, mist daily for three days. Don't load it for a week. Pull forms after 24 hours. While it's curing, head to the lumberyard and pre-stain the frame timbers — that's the next weekend's work prepped.

Pro tip ·Set your anchor bolts using a paper template cut to match the post-base footprint. Tape it to the wet concrete, push the bolts through, peel paper off when the concrete sets. Your post bases will land within ⅛″ of each other.
02
Heavy timber frame
Weekend 210 hours · 2 people essential

The most satisfying phase. By Sunday afternoon you'll be standing inside something that looks like the finished pavilion. Don't rush the squaring — everything downstream depends on this.

2.1 · Bases
Bolt down the four post bases

Simpson ABU66Z standoff bases over the anchor bolts. The standoff lifts the post ¾″ above the concrete — critical for keeping the end grain dry. Snug the nuts; don't crank yet.

2.2 · Posts
Raise the four 6×6 posts

Pre-stain all four sides before raising (much easier on the ground). Set in bases, plumb in both directions with the 4-ft level, brace temporarily with diagonal 2×4s screwed to stakes. Repeat for all four. Get them plumb and stay obsessive about it.

2.3 · Beams
Sandwich the doubled beams

Two 2×8s flank each long-side pair of posts — one on the inside face, one on the outside. They run continuous from end to end, flush with the post tops. Clamp first, drill four ½″ holes per post, drive carriage bolts through, washer-and-nut on the inside.

2.4 · Square
Diagonal-check the frame

Measure both diagonals corner-post to corner-post. If they match, you're square. If they don't, push the long diagonal in by tapping the bottom of one post sideways before the bolts are fully torqued. Then crank everything down.

2.5 · Rafters
Drop in the rafters

Seven 2×6 rafters spanning the short dimension, 22″ on center. Notch the bottom 1½″ deep × 7¼″ wide to seat over the doubled beams (called a "bird's mouth" cut). Tie down with H1 hurricane ties — two screws each side. Let the rafters overhang 12″ at front and back.

2.6 · Braces
Corner braces, all four corners

2×6 cut both ends at 45°, roughly 30″ long. Snug into the corner of post-and-beam, lag-bolt two ⅜″ × 4″ bolts at each end. These are doing real work — they triangulate the frame against racking. Stain to match.

Pro tip ·Pre-drill every bolt hole oversized by ¹⁄₁₆″. Doug fir is hard and you'll snap your driver before you get a ½″ bolt through a dry 6×6. A countersink for the bolt head also keeps things cleaner.
03
Lattice & polycarbonate roof
Weekend 36 hours · solo OK

A two-layer roof. The lower layer is open 2×2 lattice for the shadow play — it's what gives this whole thing its character. The upper layer is twin-wall polycarbonate, because this is Vancouver and we have feelings about rain.

3.1 · Slope
Confirm your roof slope

The frame as built is flat. To shed water, you need 1½–2″ of fall from front to back. Either rip a tapered shim along the top of the rear beam, or — easier — packing strips that lift the front edge of the lattice slightly. Aim for ~10° max.

3.2 · Slats
Lay the 2×2 lattice slats

Eleven slats running perpendicular to the rafters (so, the long way across the 11′ direction), spaced 12″ on center. Pre-stain. Screw down with two 2½″ screws at each rafter intersection. Let them overhang the side rafters by 2–3″ for a clean shadow line.

3.3 · Caps
Tape the polycarbonate channels

Twin-wall polycarbonate has open flutes at the ends. Tape the top edge with solid aluminum tape and the bottom edge with vented breather tape. This stops bugs and water sitting in the channels.

3.4 · Lay sheets
Drop the polycarbonate

Three 4×12 sheets cover the 11′ × 10′ roof area with 12″ overhangs front/back. UV-protected side up (it's marked). Flutes must run down-slope so condensation drains. Overlap or H-channel between sheets.

3.5 · Fasten
Screw down — but not too tight

Polycarbonate-specific screws with neoprene washers, every 18″ along each slat. Pre-drill the polycarbonate ³⁄₁₆″ oversize — the material expands and contracts a lot, and if you pin it tight it'll buckle in summer or crack in winter.

3.6 · Trim
Edge flashing

Bend a piece of pre-finished aluminum flashing over the front edge to keep wind from getting under the sheets. Caulk the back edge to the rear wall top plate (we'll get there in phase 4).

Pro tip ·Walk the lattice with weight only over rafters, never between. A 2×2 on edge will support you for a step but not a stand. Lay a sheet of plywood across two rafters as a working platform.
04
Walls & cladding
Weekend 412 hours · solo OK

Three walls — solid rear, full left, partial right. This is the weekend you turn a pergola into a room. Plan your cuts so the horizontal cladding lines wrap continuously around the inside corner.

4.1 · Frame
Wall framing — 2×4 studs

Build each wall as a stud panel on the ground: bottom plate PT, top plate SPF, studs at 16″ o.c. Match the height to the beam underside (~7′-10″). Lift and stand each panel between posts, toenail into post sides and into the slab through the PT bottom plate.

4.2 · Sheath
Plywood sheathing & wrap

½″ exterior plywood across the outside face of each wall — locks the wall against racking. Then Tyvek house wrap, taped at all seams. The wrap is what keeps wind-driven Vancouver rain from getting behind your cladding.

4.3 · Strapping
Optional rain screen

For longest life, run vertical ¾″ × 1½″ strapping over the Tyvek every 16″ before the cladding. This gives a drainage gap behind the boards. Skip if budget is tight, but you lose 5–10 years of cladding life.

4.4 · Cladding
Horizontal T&G boards

1×6 cedar or fir, tongue up, groove down. Start at the bottom, level the first row religiously — every row after follows. Blind-nail through the tongue with stainless 1¼″ finish nails so no fasteners show. Stagger butt joints on stud lines.

4.5 · Corners
Inside & outside corners

Where two walls meet, run 1×4 corner trim over the cladding edges. Caulk behind it. The exposed end-grain of the cladding is the weak point in wet weather — corner boards hide that and seal it.

4.6 · Finish
Stain

Two coats of honey-toned exterior stain on the cladding, dry to dry. Wait at least 8 hours between coats. The dark walnut stain on the frame should already be on — touch up any nicks from construction.

Pro tip ·If you can't get T&G in the species you want, shiplap is the next best thing and easier to install. The crisp shadow line at every board edge actually reads more like the reference image than a tight T&G joint.
05
Plumbing, drain & lights
Weekend 510 hours · plumber if uncertain

The shower drain and supply lines are best roughed in before the pad pour — but if you didn't, you can surface-mount everything with black iron pipe and chase the supply through a shallow channel. This is the weekend the building becomes useful.

5.1 · Drain
Connect the drain

Linear drain at the low point of the shower zone. Run a 2″ ABS line under the pad (if pre-poured) or trench around the outside to your existing storm drain. Vent if your local code requires it — a 1¼″ AAV up inside the partial right wall works.

5.2 · Supply
Hot & cold supply lines

½″ PEX-A from your house — red for hot, blue for cold. Bury 24″ deep below frost line for the run from the house. Pop up inside the partial right wall, leave 18″ of pigtail at shower height.

5.3 · Valve
Install the mixing valve

Thermostatic mixing valve (Caleffi 521 or equivalent) inside the wall cavity, hot and cold in, tempered out. Set to 105°F. This is your anti-scald — non-negotiable if you ever hook up solar pre-heat later.

5.4 · Fixture
Build the black-pipe shower

¾″ black iron pipe assembled with elbows: floor flange, 80″ riser, 90° to a 12″ horizontal arm, then the copper rain head. Thread-seal every joint. Spray-paint matte black where the pipe was scratched. Anchor the floor flange to the pad with concrete screws.

5.5 · Plunge
Set the plunge tub

Stock tank on a level pad of its own (3″ above main pad helps with overflow). Clad the outside in vertical 1×4 boards matching the frame stain. Drain plumbed to the same line as the shower. Fill manually from the cold supply, or plumb in a dedicated cold tap.

5.6 · Lights
String lights & GFCI outlet

Outdoor S14 string lights along the underside of the lattice — staple the cable along a rafter, drape between. One GFCI-protected exterior outlet on the partial right wall, run from the house circuit. If you don't have an outdoor circuit, a licensed electrician for this part.

Pro tip ·Pressure-test the supply lines for 24 hours before closing up the wall cavity. PEX joints fail at the connections, never in the middle of a run — and you want any leak to be obvious now, not three months from now when it has soaked your sheathing.
06
Fit-out & ritual
Weekend 66 hours · enjoy

The pieces that turn a building into a place. Bench, hooks, the river rock band, the small things you'll forget you needed until you stand inside the finished thing for the first time.

6.1 · Step
Build the step bench

A simple box of 2×4 frame clad in 1×6 boards matching the plunge cladding. Sized to land at half the plunge height — 12″ tall, 18″ × 24″ footprint. Position so it's a comfortable step in and a usable bench when you're not getting in.

6.2 · Rock
River rock perimeter

Spread the 1–2″ river rock in the 12″ collar around the pad. About 0.5 yd³ for the full perimeter. It catches splash, deters mud, and makes the building feel like it sits in a landscape rather than on top of one.

6.3 · Hooks
Towel hooks & the small stuff

Two or three matte-black hooks on the inside face of the left wall, about 60″ off the pad. A small open shelf for soap and salt. A peg for the bath thermometer. These details cost almost nothing and matter every time.

6.4 · Stain
Final stain touch-ups

Walk the whole structure and touch up every nick from construction. The frame's dark walnut should be saturated and even. The cladding's honey tone should be uniform front-to-back. Don't skip the cut ends of the rafter tails.

6.5 · Test
Run a full cycle

Fill the plunge, get the shower hot, sit on the bench. Find what's missing. A hook in the wrong place, a step that wobbles, a slat that vibrates in the wind. Fix those before you call it done.

6.6 · Use
Use it tonight

This is the most important step. Buildings that don't get used in their first week tend not to get used. Make a fire in a portable pit nearby, light the string lights, run the shower hot, and step into the cold. You're done.

Pro tip ·Buy a single good bath sheet — a proper thick cotton or linen one — and keep it in a sealed bin inside the pavilion. The ritual works better when the towel is right there waiting.
