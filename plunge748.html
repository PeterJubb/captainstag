A backyard bath pavilion · the build plan

Onsen,
by hand.

An 11 × 8 outdoor bath pavilion alongside the existing sauna. Heavy timber frame stained dark, fir cladding on the outside only — exposed framing inside. Black corrugated tin roof to match the sauna. A cold plunge, a hot shower, and zero grid connection.

Site & services Built on the existing concrete pad — no excavation, no slab pour. No house electrical run: propane on-demand heater for hot, garden hose from the bib for cold, a black hose coil on the roof for solar pre-heat, and solar string lights for evenings.

A2.2 Water
Drawings · 05

How the water works.

Cold from the garden hose splits two ways: half goes direct to the cold side of the mixing valve, half runs through the black hose coiled on the roof and into the propane heater. The heater finishes the job to a steady setpoint. Tempering valve at the fixture, set to 105°F.

Schematic · not to scale
A2.2   Water schematic — solar pre-heat + propane finishing
Why this works ·The roof coil isn't enough on its own — Vancouver doesn't deliver shower-hot water from a hose every day. But it does the easy half of the work for free in summer, and the propane heater only has to finish the lift the rest of the time. Result: propane consumption drops by 50–70% in July–August, and the system still works on a January morning.

A4.0 Build
The build · 08

Five phases, in order.

Frame first, and only after the frame is plumb, square, and bolted does anything else go up. Roof before walls so you stay dry while you finish the rest. Water and fit-out last.

01
Heavy timber frame

Lay out on the existing slab, drill in your anchors, raise four 6×6 posts, then drop the doubled 2×8 beams across the top.

1.1 · Layout
Mark the four post positions

Sweep the existing slab clean. Snap chalk lines at 11′-0″ × 8′-0″, square the rectangle with a diagonal check (13′-7½″ across the corners). Mark the center of each post base.

1.2 · Anchors
Drill & epoxy the anchor bolts

Hammer drill ⅝″ holes 5″ deep at each post position. Blow out the dust, inject Hilti HIT-HY 200 epoxy, drop in ⅝″ anchor rods. Let cure per spec (~1 hour). Then bolt down the four Simpson ABU66Z standoff bases.

1.3 · Posts
Raise the four 6×6 posts

Pre-stain all four sides before raising — easier on the ground. Two posts at full 8′-0″ (front), two cut to 7′-0″ (back) for the mono-slope. Set in bases, plumb both directions, brace temporarily with diagonal 2×4s.

1.4 · Beams
Sandwich the doubled beams

Two 2×8s flank each long-side pair of posts — one inside, one outside, flush with post tops. They run continuous, sloping front-high to back-low. Clamp, drill four ½″ holes per post, drive carriage bolts through.

1.5 · Square
Diagonal-check before final torque

Measure both corner-to-corner diagonals. Match within ⅛″ or push the long one in by tapping the bottom of a post sideways. Only then crank everything down to spec.

1.6 · Braces
Corner braces, all four corners

2×6 cut both ends at 45°, roughly 30″ on the long edge. Snug into the corner of post-and-beam, lag-bolt two ⅜″ × 4″ at each end. These triangulate the frame against racking. Stain to match.

Pro tip ·Pre-drill every bolt hole oversized by ¹⁄₁₆″. Doug fir is hard, and you'll snap an impact driver before getting a ½″ bolt through a dry 6×6 otherwise.
02
Black tin roof

Mono-slope, matte black corrugated steel — matching the sauna. Rafters take the slope. Purlins on top. Panels screw down to the purlins.

2.1 · Rafters
Drop in the rafters

Six 2×6 rafters spanning the 8′ depth, 22″ on center. Notch the bottom 1½″ × 5½″ at each end to seat over the doubled beams (a bird's-mouth cut). Tie down with Simpson H1 hurricane ties — two screws per side. Let rafters overhang 6″ at front and back.

2.2 · Purlins
Lay the purlins

2×4 purlins perpendicular to the rafters, 24″ on center. Four runs across the 8′ depth. Two 2½″ screws at each rafter intersection. Pre-stain dark walnut — they'll be visible from below.

2.3 · Drip edge
Install drip edge at the low end

Black aluminum drip edge runs along the back (low) edge, screwed to the last purlin. Catches water off the back of the panels and throws it clear of the rear wall.

2.4 · First panel
Set the first panel square

Start at the side opposite prevailing wind (in Vancouver, start east, lap west) so laps shed wind-driven rain. Panel overhangs the front edge 2″ and the back edge 1½″. Once the first panel is true, the rest follow.

2.5 · Lap & screw
Subsequent panels — one corrugation overlap

Each panel overlaps the previous by one full corrugation. Screw down with metal roofing screws + neoprene washers, at the bottom of every other corrugation, every 18″ along each purlin. Snug — not so tight you deform the washer.

2.6 · Trim out
Foam closures & rake trim

Foam closure strips into the bottom corrugations at the top edge. Black rake trim along the side edges. A bead of butyl seam tape under each lap joint if you want belt-and-suspenders for wind-driven rain.

Pro tip ·Don't walk on the panels. Lay a sheet of plywood across two purlins as a kneeler. Standing on metal between purlins dents it permanently — and dents catch water exactly where you don't want them.
03
Walls & cladding

Three walls — full rear, full left, partial right behind the shower. Open south face stays open. Cladding goes on the outside only; inside is left as visible stud framing. The honest, sauna-adjacent look.

3.1 · Frame walls
Build each wall as a stud panel

Bottom plate PT, top plate SPF, studs at 16″ o.c. Match the height to the beam underside (~7′-10″ at the front, less at the back). Build flat on the slab first, then lift and stand between posts.

3.2 · Anchor & toenail
Fasten walls down & in

Wedge-anchor the PT bottom plate to the existing slab — ¼″ × 3″ anchors every 24″. Toenail wall studs at the ends into the posts with 3″ structural screws. Plumb each wall as you go.

3.3 · Wrap
Tyvek on the outside face of the studs

Staple Tyvek across the outside face of each wall, taped at seams. The wrap is the only thing between the stud cavity and weather — there's no plywood sheathing. The pavilion's main corner braces and hurricane ties handle lateral loads instead.

3.4 · Cladding
Horizontal fir T&G boards

1×6 fir T&G, tongue up, groove down. Start at the bottom and level the first row religiously — every row above follows. Blind-nail through the tongue with stainless 1¼″ finish nails directly into the studs. No fasteners show.

3.5 · Corners
Trim the corners

Where two walls meet, run 1×4 fir corner trim over the cladding edges, caulked behind. The exposed end-grain of the cladding is the weak point in wet weather — corner boards hide and seal it.

3.6 · Finish
Stain

Two coats of honey-toned exterior stain on the cladding outside, dry to dry, 8 hr between. Walnut on the frame should already be on from phase 1 — touch up any nicks. The inside studs get one coat of clear penetrating sealer (they'll see splash).

Pro tip ·Fir T&G is half the price of cedar and stains beautifully — it just needs the stain to seal it against rot. Skip the stain step at your peril; bare fir in Vancouver weather is dead in three winters.
04
Water — propane, solar & the drain

Cold comes from a garden hose off the house bib. Half the cold runs through a black hose coiled on the roof for solar pre-heat, then feeds the propane heater. Hot from the heater + cold direct, mixed at a tempering valve at the fixture. Drain sits on top of the existing slab.

4.1 · Drain
Surface-mount the linear drain

Linear drain sits on the slab along the back of the shower zone, against the rear wall. The 2″ ABS line runs along the slab base behind the partial right wall, exits at the back corner, and outfalls to grade in the alley or your existing storm drain.

4.2 · Cold supply
Garden hose from house bib

50 ft of ¾″ garden hose from the house bib runs along the fence line or ground (or buried in conduit if you want it tidier) into the pavilion. Enters near the west wall at slab level. Brass Y-splitter immediately inside: one branch up to the roof, one branch direct to the mixing valve cold inlet.

4.3 · Solar coil
Lay 100′ of black hose on the roof

Spiral 100 ft of ¾″ black garden hose flat on top of the corrugated metal panels, secured every 18″ with stainless zip-ties through small holes in the high ridges (caulk after). Inlet at the south (high, sun-facing) end of the coil, outlet at the north feeding down to the propane heater. Black hose + black tin = 90–110°F output on a sunny summer day.

4.4 · Propane heater
Mount the tankless heater

Heavy-duty black wall brackets on the exterior face of the partial right wall, near the rear. Heater outlet ~4 ft off the slab. Vent clearance per manufacturer (typically 12″ above, 6″ each side). Hose-thread inlet from the solar coil; hose-thread outlet runs as ¾″ PEX-A back inside the wall cavity to the mixing valve. Propane tank sits on the slab beside it in a small screen of slatted wood matching the plunge.

4.5 · Mixing valve & fixture
Black-pipe shower on the east wall

Thermostatic mixing valve inside the partial right wall cavity — hot in from the heater, cold in from the Y-splitter, tempered out at 105°F. From there ¾″ PEX-A to the shower fixture. Black iron pipe floor flange anchored to the slab against the east wall, 80″ vertical riser, 90° elbow with a 12″ horizontal arm extending west, copper rain head at the west end pointing down. You stand under the head facing east — back to the plunge, facing the sauna wall.

4.6 · Plunge & lights
Set the plunge & hang the solar string

Stock tank on a 2×4 base lifting it ~3″ off the slab. Clad the outside in vertical 1×4 fir stained dark walnut. Plumb the drain to the same line as the shower. Fill manually via the cold hose, or tee in a dedicated cold tap. Solar string lights: panel zip-tied to the south-facing high edge of the roof in full sun, cable draped along the underside of the purlins. They auto-on at dusk.

Pro tip ·The propane heater needs to draw water at a minimum flow rate (~0.6 gpm typical) to fire. If solar pre-heat overshoots and the water arrives too hot, the heater won't fire and the tempering valve handles the mix on its own. That's fine. The whole system is self-balancing.
Winter note ·The solar coil, garden hoses, and tempering valve all live outside and will freeze. From November to March, drain the system: disconnect the hose at the house bib, open the valve at the lowest point, let everything bleed. The propane heater has its own winterization steps in its manual. The pavilion stays usable in winter only if you're comfortable with quick cold-water rinses.
05
Fit-out & ritual

The pieces that turn a building into a place. Step bench, river rock band, hooks. The small things you'll forget you needed until you stand inside the finished thing for the first time.

5.1 · Step
Build the step bench

A simple box of 2×4 frame clad in 1×4 boards matching the plunge cladding. 12″ tall, 18″ × 24″ footprint. Position alongside the plunge so it's a comfortable step in and a usable bench when you're not getting in.

5.2 · Rock
River rock perimeter band

Spread 1–2″ river rock in a 12″ band around the slab perimeter. Catches splash, deters mud, blurs the hard edge between the new building and the old pad it sits on.

5.3 · Hooks
Towel hooks & the small stuff

Two or three matte-black hooks on a stud face of the left wall, ~60″ off the slab. A small open shelf for soap and salt. A peg for the bath thermometer. These details cost almost nothing and matter every time.

5.4 · Stain touch-ups
Final pass on the finish

Walk the whole structure. Walnut on the frame should be saturated and even. Honey on cladding should be uniform front-to-back. Don't skip the cut ends of the rafter tails — that's where weather gets in first.

5.5 · Test
Run a full cycle

Open the house bib, fire the propane heater, fill the plunge cold, get the shower hot, sit on the bench. Find what's missing — a hook in the wrong place, a step that wobbles, a purlin that ticks in the wind. Fix those before you call it done.

5.6 · Use
Use it tonight

The most important step. Buildings that don't get used in their first week tend not to get used. Light the strings, run the sauna, run the shower hot, step into the cold. You're done.

Pro tip ·Buy one good bath sheet — proper thick cotton or linen — and keep it in a sealed bin inside the pavilion. The ritual works better when the towel is right there waiting.
